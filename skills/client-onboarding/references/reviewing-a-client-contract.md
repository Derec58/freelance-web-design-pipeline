# Reviewing a Contract the Client Sends You

Most local clients won't have their own contract — your agreement (see `contract-clauses.md`) is the default and the cleaner option. But established businesses, agencies, and [a higher-cost metro] companies sometimes send their own **contractor agreement, MSA (Master Service Agreement), SOW (Statement of Work), or terms** and ask you to sign.

**Never sign it as-is on the spot.** Read it from your side first. These documents are almost always written to protect *them*, and a few standard-looking clauses can quietly put a freelancer at real risk. Below is what to look for, why it matters, and what to ask for instead.

**This is practical guidance, not legal advice.** For deals over ~$5,000, international clients (different legal systems entirely), or anything with unusual IP or indemnity language you don't fully understand, have an actual attorney look before you sign. A one-time contract review is cheap insurance.

---

## How to do the review

1. Read the whole thing once, slowly. Note anything you don't understand.
2. Go through the trap list below and flag each clause that's present.
3. Don't negotiate every line — pick the few that actually expose you and propose specific changes. Reasonable clients expect a freelancer to redline a contract; it signals you're a professional, not a pushover.
4. Send your requested changes politely and in writing (a tracked-changes doc or a short numbered list both work).

A clean way to open the conversation:
> "Thanks for sending this over — happy to work under an agreement. I went through it and have a few small adjustments from my side so it works for both of us. Nothing unusual — mostly around payment timing, scope, and IP transferring on final payment. Can I send those back?"

---

## The traps to flag

### 1. IP transfers before (or regardless of) payment
**Look for:** language assigning all work product / "work made for hire" to the client on signing or on delivery, with no link to payment.
**Why it matters:** if they own the work before they've paid, your only leverage to get paid is gone.
**Ask for:** ownership transfers **upon receipt of final payment**. Until then, you retain all rights and they may not deploy or use the deliverables.

### 2. Your reusable tools and pre-existing work get swept up
**Look for:** broad IP assignment that would hand over your frameworks, components, snippets, or anything you built before this project.
**Why it matters:** you reuse your own building blocks across clients — you can't assign those away.
**Ask for:** you retain ownership of your **pre-existing / background IP and general know-how**, and grant the client a license to use it as part of the delivered site.

### 3. No right to show the work in your portfolio
**Look for:** confidentiality or IP clauses that, read literally, would stop you from displaying the project anywhere — or an explicit "contractor may not reference this engagement."
**Why it matters:** your entire client pipeline runs on case studies and social proof. Losing portfolio rights quietly kneecaps your marketing.
**Ask for:** an explicit clause that you **may display the work in your portfolio and case studies** (offer a delay or anonymization if it's genuinely sensitive). This one is worth holding firm on.

### 4. Open-ended revisions / "to client's satisfaction"
**Look for:** acceptance tied to the client's subjective satisfaction, or no cap on revisions.
**Why it matters:** unlimited rework with no definition of "done" — you can work forever and never get to final payment.
**Ask for:** a defined number of revision rounds and **objective acceptance criteria** (deliverable matches the agreed scope/SOW); extra rounds billed hourly.

### 5. Vague or expanding scope
**Look for:** "and related services," "as reasonably requested," or no attached deliverable list.
**Why it matters:** scope creep with a fixed price.
**Ask for:** a specific **SOW/exhibit listing exact deliverables**; anything beyond it is a written change order.

### 6. Bad payment terms
**Look for:** net-30/60/90, "payment upon final acceptance" only, or no deposit.
**Why it matters:** you'd be financing their project and waiting months to get paid, with all the collection risk.
**Ask for:** **50% deposit before work begins**, balance net-7 to net-15 (or milestone payments on larger jobs). Push back hard on anything beyond net-30.

### 7. Termination for convenience with no kill fee
**Look for:** client may terminate "at any time for any reason" with payment only for work "accepted" or "completed."
**Why it matters:** they cancel mid-project and you eat the reserved time and unfinished work.
**Ask for:** on cancellation, payment for **all work done to date plus a cancellation fee** (e.g., 25% of the remaining balance), and the **deposit is non-refundable**.

### 8. Broad indemnification
**Look for:** you agreeing to "defend, indemnify, and hold harmless" the client against essentially everything.
**Why it matters:** you could be on the hook for their legal costs over things outside your control (their content, their business claims, third-party tools).
**Ask for:** narrow it to claims arising from **your own IP infringement or gross negligence**, make it **mutual**, and cap it. Strike open-ended indemnities.

### 9. No limitation of liability / uncapped liability
**Look for:** no liability cap, or you being liable for indirect/consequential damages (lost profits, business interruption).
**Why it matters:** unlimited exposure far beyond what they paid you.
**Ask for:** total liability **capped at the fees paid** under the agreement, with **indirect and consequential damages excluded**.

### 10. Non-compete or exclusivity
**Look for:** you can't work with similar businesses, competitors, or in their industry for some period.
**Why it matters:** as a freelancer serving many local businesses, this can choke your livelihood.
**Ask for:** strike it, or narrow it drastically — a specific named competitor and a short, defined window at most.

### 11. Guaranteeing business results
**Look for:** warranties that the site will "increase sales," "rank #1," or hit specific traffic/revenue numbers.
**Why it matters:** you can't control their market, ad spend, or operations — don't promise outcomes you can't guarantee.
**Ask for:** warrant your **workmanship** (the site functions as specified) for a defined period; **no guarantee of business results.**

### 12. Unilateral changes
**Look for:** "client may modify these terms at any time."
**Why it matters:** the deal you signed isn't the deal anymore.
**Ask for:** changes require **mutual written agreement.**

## Protections to add if their contract is missing them

Their document is built to protect them, so it often omits the things that protect *you*. If they're not present, ask to add:
- A **deposit** before work begins
- A **kill fee / cancellation** term and a non-refundable deposit
- **Late-payment fees**
- A **client-responsibilities / delay clause** (project pauses if they go dark)
- A **liability cap**
- **Portfolio display rights**

You can lift the exact language for any of these from `contract-clauses.md`.

---

## When to walk

If a client refuses every reasonable adjustment — insists on owning IP without paying, no deposit, unlimited revisions, and a non-compete — that tells you how the whole project will go. A confident pass is fine: better no project than one structured so you can't win.
