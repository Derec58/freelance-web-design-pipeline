# Red Flag Signals

Clients are clients and most deserve a fair shot. These signals don't automatically mean say no — they mean go in with tighter agreements, clearer scope, and specific expectations set upfront. The response scripts below are how to handle each situation professionally.

---

## Signals and How to Respond

**"I just need something simple and cheap."**

Almost always means no budget and expanding scope the moment you start. Get specific immediately.

> "Totally understand — let me make sure I scope this right. What pages do you need, and what's the main thing you want visitors to do? Once I know that I can give you a realistic number."

Nail down scope before agreeing to anything. Put it in the agreement explicitly.

---

**"I'll know what I want when I see it."**

Means no clear direction and unlimited revision expectations. Reframe the process.

> "That's totally fine — that's actually what the questionnaire and wireframe review stage is for. We get aligned on direction before I build anything, so by the time you see the first design you've already approved the overall approach. It works really well."

Make sure the contract defines what a revision round is and what it is not.

---

**"My last designer was terrible."**

Sometimes true. Sometimes a pattern. Ask for specifics.

> "Sorry to hear that — what specifically went wrong? I want to make sure we set things up differently from the start."

If they can't say specifically what went wrong, or if every designer they've worked with was "terrible," that's a pattern. Proceed with tighter scope and more documented approvals at each stage.

---

**"Can you match this exact design?"**

Copying another site is a copyright and legal problem.

> "I can take inspiration from what you like about it — the layout, the feel, the color approach — but I can't replicate someone else's design directly. What I'll do is capture what you love about it and build something original that has that same energy. Works better for your brand anyway."

---

**Refuses to share any budget.**

Not a dealbreaker, but budget-less conversations often lead to proposals that miss the mark entirely.

> "Even a rough range helps me scope what's realistic — are we talking a few hundred, a few thousand, or more? I want to make sure I'm not over- or under-building for what you actually need."

If they still won't engage, give them your pricing table from the proposal section and ask which tier feels right.

---

**Wants everything done yesterday.**

Rushed timelines create rushed work and unhappy clients on both sides.

> "I can move quickly, but good work takes some focused time. What's driving the timeline — is there a specific event or deadline? Let's see if we can find a target that works."

If the timeline is genuinely impossible, say so clearly and propose what is achievable. Don't commit to something you can't deliver.

---

**Multiple decision-makers with no clear lead.**

"I need to run it by my wife/partner/manager" is fine. Multiple stakeholders with no designated decision-maker means stalled approvals and conflicting feedback.

Ask during the discovery call: "Who else is involved in this decision?" and "Who has final say?"

If there are multiple people, ask to have the decision-maker on the call or copy them on the proposal. Don't design by committee without knowing who the tiebreaker is.

If they refuse to designate a single lead, put it in the agreement: "All feedback must be consolidated and submitted as a single message by [Name] as the designated project contact. Conflicting feedback from multiple stakeholders that requires rework will be treated as a change order." This doesn't solve the problem but it protects you.

---

**Excessive haggling over price.**

Negotiating a little is normal. Trying to cut the price by 50% before you've even started signals they don't respect the value of the work.

> "I understand budget is a real consideration. Here's what I can do — if we simplify the scope to [fewer pages / no CMS / fewer features], I can bring the investment down to $[lower number]. But I can't maintain the same quality at a significantly lower price for the same scope. Which direction makes more sense for you?"

If they won't budge and the price they want isn't worth your time, it's okay to pass.

---

**They want to pay in installments beyond the standard 50/50 split.**

"Can I pay a third upfront, a third halfway, and a third at the end?" sounds reasonable but creates real problems — you're essentially financing their project, and the final payment becomes harder to collect as leverage disappears the moment you've handed anything over.

> "My standard is 50% to start and 50% on delivery — that's the structure I use for every project because it keeps things simple and fair for both sides. The deposit covers my time in the design phase, and the final payment clears before any files transfer or the site goes live. Does that work for you?"

If they push back hard, it's a signal they're worried about the budget — which is worth surfacing directly rather than accommodating with a payment plan that puts you at risk.

---

**They want you to start before signing or paying the deposit.**

This is a firm no. No exceptions.

> "I totally get the excitement to get started — I feel the same way. But my process requires a signed agreement and deposit before I block time for a project. It protects both of us. I can have the agreement to you today and we can be started as soon as the deposit clears."

If they push back on this, that's itself a red flag.

---

**They ask you to do something legally or ethically problematic.**

Examples: copy a competitor's design directly, use unlicensed images, make claims about their service that aren't true, spam-link build for SEO.

> "I can't do that one — [brief reason]. What I can do is [compliant alternative that achieves the same goal]. Want me to go that route?"

Your name is on the work. Don't let a client's bad idea become your liability.

---

## When to Walk Away

Most situations above can be managed with clear agreements and upfront conversations. But it is okay to decline a project if:

- The client is openly disrespectful or dismissive during the discovery call
- The budget is so far below your minimum that it isn't worth the time even for a portfolio piece
- The scope is so undefined that a fixed-price agreement is impossible to protect yourself with
- The client has already worked with multiple designers who all "did a bad job" and can't explain why
- They want you to do something you're not comfortable with and won't accept an alternative

A confident, professional pass: "Based on our conversation, I don't think I'm the right fit for this project — I want to be upfront about that so you can find someone who's a better match. Good luck with it."

You don't have to explain further. No guilt.
