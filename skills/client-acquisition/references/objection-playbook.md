# Objection Playbook

How to handle the pushbacks a local business owner actually gives you — on a call, in a DM, or in person. Adapted from sales objection playbooks and rewritten for local web design.

**The one rule that governs all of these:** your goal is never to "win" the objection. It's to either (a) earn the 15-minute call, or (b) leave the door open and the relationship intact. A polite no today is a referral or a callback in six months. Never argue, never pressure, never get defensive.

For each objection below: what they say → why they're really saying it → how to respond.

---

## Money

### "How much does it cost?"
**Why:** Reasonable question, but answering with a number before you understand their needs anchors the whole conversation on price and usually ends it.
**Response:**
> "It really depends on what you need — every project is different. That's why I'd love a quick 15-minute call first, just to understand your business. Then I can give you a realistic sense of what makes sense. Does [day] work?"

### "That's too expensive" / "I can't afford that"
**Why:** Either a real budget ceiling, or they don't yet see the return. Don't drop your price reflexively — reframe to value or offer a smaller starting scope.
**Response:**
> "Totally fair. The way I think about it: a site that brings in even one or two extra jobs a month usually pays for itself pretty quickly. But I also don't want to push something that doesn't fit — if budget's the concern, we can start with a smaller first phase and build from there. Want me to put together what that could look like?"

### "Can you just do it cheaper?"
**Why:** Testing whether your price is firm. Discounting on demand signals your first number was inflated.
**Response:**
> "I keep my pricing honest, so there's not a lot of padding to cut. What I can do is adjust the scope — we focus on what'll move the needle most for you now, and leave the nice-to-haves for later. That keeps it affordable without me cutting corners on quality."

## "I'll do it another way"

### "My nephew / cousin / friend can build one" / "I'll just use Wix or Squarespace myself"
**Why:** They see a website as a commodity, not as something that performs. This is your single best moment to draw the line between a template and a site built to get found and convert.
**Response:**
> "That can absolutely work to get something up. The difference is usually in whether it actually brings in customers — showing up on Google when someone searches [service] in [city], loading fast on a phone, and being easy to contact. A lot of DIY sites look fine but don't do that part. No pressure at all — but if you want, I can take a look at what you've got or what you're planning and tell you honestly whether it'll get you there."

### "I get all my business from word of mouth / Facebook / referrals"
**Why:** It's working for them, so the need feels low. Don't dismiss what's working — add to it.
**Response:**
> "That's great, and honestly word of mouth is the best kind of business. Here's the thing though — when someone hears your name, the first thing a lot of them do is Google you before they call. If there's nothing there, or the site looks dated, it can quietly cost you the people referrals are already sending. A site doesn't replace word of mouth — it catches the people it sends your way."

## Past experience

### "I tried a website before and it didn't do anything"
**Why:** They've been burned — probably by a template nobody optimized or maintained. They're skeptical, not closed.
**Response:**
> "I hear that a lot, and usually it's because the site got built and then just… sat there — nothing done to help it show up on Google or actually convert visitors. That's the part that matters. If you're open to it, I'd like to look at what you had and show you specifically what was missing. No obligation."

---

## Stalls

### "Just send me some info" / "Email me your prices"
**Why:** Usually a polite brush-off, but sometimes genuine. A pricing sheet with no context gets ignored or used to bargain-shop. Convert it back to a short conversation.
**Response:**
> "Happy to. Honestly though, anything I send without understanding your business would just be a generic number that probably isn't accurate for you. Could we do 10–15 minutes so I can send you something that actually fits? I'll keep it quick."

### "I need to think about it" / "I have to talk to my partner"
**Why:** Could be real, could be a soft no. Don't pressure — make the next step concrete and easy.
**Response:**
> "Of course — it's a real decision and you should take your time. Would it help if I put together a quick summary of what I found and what I'd suggest, so you've got something concrete to talk through? Then I'll follow up [specific day] — no pressure either way."

### "I'm too busy right now"
**Why:** Genuinely busy, or deflecting. Respect it and pin down a real time.
**Response:**
> "No problem at all — when's usually a better time to reach you? I can work around your schedule, even if it's a few weeks out."

## Trust / credibility

### "How do I know you're any good?" / "Can I see your work?"
**Why:** Completely fair, especially from a cold contact. This is where social proof does the work — never get defensive about it.
**Response:**
> "Absolutely — you should see what you'd be getting. [Share your strongest example / live site you built / dereckvilla.design once live.] I'm local, so I'm not going anywhere — and I'm happy to meet in person so you know exactly who you're working with. Want me to walk you through a couple of examples on a quick call?"

*(If you don't have local case studies yet: be honest. "I'm early in building my local portfolio, which is part of why my pricing is fair right now — but here's my best work and exactly what I'd do for you." Honesty converts skeptics better than bluffing.)*

---

## The clean no

### "Not interested"
**Why:** Sometimes it's a real no. Accept it gracefully — your reputation in a small local market is worth more than one pushed deal.
**Response:**
> "No problem — I appreciate you taking the time. If you ever want a second opinion on your web presence down the road, feel free to reach out. Have a great day."

Then stop. Mark it as a no. Local owners talk to each other — the way you handle a no is its own kind of marketing.

---

## After the conversation

Whatever the outcome, log it in `project.md` (or your prospect tracker if they didn't convert): what they objected to, what you said, and whether to revisit. Patterns in the objections you hear most often should feed back into your outreach — if three plumbers in a row say "word of mouth is fine," your opening line to plumbers should pre-empt it.
